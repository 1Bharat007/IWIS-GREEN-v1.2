# Composting and Organic Waste

## What is Composting?
Composting is the natural process of recycling organic matter, such as leaves and food scraps, into a valuable fertilizer that can enrich soil and plants.

## Composting Methods
1. **Home/Backyard Composting:** Creating a compost pile or using a compost bin in a yard or balcony.
2. **Community Composting:** A shared composting site for a neighborhood or apartment complex.
3. **Vermicomposting:** Using specific species of earthworms (like Red Wigglers) to break down organic material rapidly. Worm castings are an exceptionally rich fertilizer.
4. **Aerobic Composting:** Decomposition in the presence of oxygen. Requires turning the pile regularly. Fast and does not smell if done correctly.
5. **Anaerobic Digestion:** Decomposition without oxygen, usually in a sealed bio-digester. Produces biogas (methane) which can be used for cooking or electricity.

## Step-by-Step Guide to Home Composting
1. **Choose a Container/Spot:** A bin with ventilation holes or a designated spot in the yard.
2. **Layering (Browns and Greens):**
   *   **Greens (Nitrogen-rich):** Vegetable/fruit scraps, coffee grounds, fresh grass clippings.
   *   **Browns (Carbon-rich):** Dry leaves, twigs, shredded cardboard/paper, eggshells.
   *   *Rule of thumb:* Maintain a ratio of roughly 2 or 3 parts Browns to 1 part Greens by volume.
3. **Moisture:** The pile should feel like a damp sponge. Add water if too dry; add more Browns if too wet.
4. **Aeration:** Turn or stir the pile every 1-2 weeks to provide oxygen to the microbes.
5. **Harvest:** The compost is ready when it looks, feels, and smells like rich, dark soil (usually 2-6 months).

## What NOT to Compost
*   Meat, bones, and fish (attracts pests, causes odor).
*   Dairy products (cheese, butter, milk).
*   Oily or greasy foods.
*   Diseased plants or weeds with seeds.
*   Pet waste (dog/cat feces can contain harmful pathogens).
*   Glossy or coated paper.

## Troubleshooting Common Mistakes
*   **Bad Odor:** Usually means the pile is too wet or has too much nitrogen (Greens). Add dry Browns and turn the pile to aerate.
*   **Pile is Not Heating Up/Decomposing:** Usually means it's too dry, too small, or lacks nitrogen. Add Greens and water.
*   **Attracting Pests:** Ensure food scraps are buried deep in the center of the pile and covered with Browns. Do not compost meat or dairy.
