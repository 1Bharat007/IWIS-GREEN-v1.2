# Recycler Economics

## The Business of Recycling
Recycling is fundamentally a commodities business. Recyclers purchase raw material (scrap), process it, and sell it to manufacturers. The viability of recycling depends heavily on market prices for commodities like virgin plastic, oil, and metal ore.

## Material Value and Grades
*   **Metals:** Aluminum and copper are highly valuable. Steel is lower value but easy to sort using magnets.
*   **Paper:** Cardboard (OCC) and white office paper have high value. Mixed paper has lower value.
*   **Plastics:** PET (water bottles) and HDPE (milk jugs) are in high demand. Mixed plastics (grades 3-7) have very low or negative value (it costs money to recycle them).

## Collection Economics
The biggest cost in recycling is logistics (collection and transportation).
*   **High Volume, Low Weight:** Plastics take up a lot of space but weigh very little, making transport expensive per ton. Compaction and baling are essential.
*   **Contamination:** If a batch of recyclables is contaminated with food waste, it ruins the value of the entire batch.

## The Role of the Informal Sector
In many developing nations, the informal sector (waste pickers, kabadiwalas) forms the backbone of the recycling supply chain. They manually sort and aggregate high-value recyclables. Platforms like IWIS aim to formalize and uplift this sector by providing direct market access.

## Material Recovery Facilities (MRFs)
Large-scale plants that receive, separate, and prepare recyclable materials for marketing to end-user manufacturers. They require significant capital investment for optical sorters, ballistic separators, and balers.
